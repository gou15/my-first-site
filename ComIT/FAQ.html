How did Kaizo get started?
A long long time ago (2007), a Japanese fellow by the name of Takemoto built a Mario game for his roommate called Kaizo Mario World. This game ended up appearing on YouTube under the name Asshole Mario. As the video gained more and more views, eventually the ROM was uploaded online and shared. At a big speedrunning event, AGDQ 2015, a runner by the screenname Dram55 played the Kaizo game in front of a massive crowd. Kaizo Mario World was not the first hack, nor the first hard hack, but it was the first one to gain traction and noteriety as a polished and playable hack. Kaizo type games continued to make their way into the mainstream with the release of Mario Maker and(1) PangeaPanga's(2) level(3) P-break(4). P-Break was released, the fandom went crazy, Kaizo levels in Mario Maker hit the mainstream, and a subset of that mainstream fanbase turned to SMW Kaizo hacks for a different type of challenge. You can play P-Break yourself if you have access to a copy of Super Mario Maker for WiiU. Enter level code 6059-0000-005E-4FB5. Good luck!

Is any of this legal?
The short answer is yes. The longer answer is "check your local laws." In Canada and the United States, everything contained on and linked to from this site is legal. Emulators are legal. The tools used to header and patch ROMs are legal. Having a digital copy of a ROM from a game that you physically and legally own (i.e. you've purchased the game and it is still in your possession) is legal. If you came here to download ROMs or to get directed to where you can download ROMs then this site won't help you. This site will show you how to download the patches that you can apply to your own legally obtained ROM file. It is not legal to share ROMs with people who do not legally own a copy of the game, nor is it legal to sell your ROM hacks as they make use of trademarked assets. Kapiche? Don't share ROM files and you'll be fine.

Why not just use Mario Maker?
Lots of Kaizo SMW players also play Kaizo levels in Mario Maker (available on the WiiU, 3DS, and Nintendo Switch). There are 2 big reasons to play Kaizo SMW instead of Mario Maker:
  • Game physics - playing on the Super Mario World setting in Mario Maker and actually playing Super Mario World are very different. They feel different, the way Mario acts and controls is different, and the way Mario interacts with objects and other sprites is different.
  • Limitations - the limitations of Mario Maker are set by Nintendo. They'll dictate what enemies, items, backdrops, tilesets, etc... will be in the game and how those things behave. Because Kaizo ROM hacks are built by the community in a builder built by the community, and because that builder allows the input of custom code outside of the normal limitations of the original Super Mario World, the options and possibilities for level creation are limitless. Want Mario to look like Bob Ross and play in a world where the backgrounds are all Bob Ross paintings? There's a hack for that.

Why is it so hard? Do people really enjoy this?
Honestly, not a lot of people get enjoyment out of playing Kaizo Mario... I recommend trying it, but it probably isn't for you. But the people who like Kaizo really really like Kaizo. And lots of them post videos of them playing various Kaizo hacks. And most people in the Kaizo communities are the people who just enjoy watching and talking about Kaizo Mario. You don't have to be a football player to love the NFL, just like you don't need to play Kaizo to love watching Kaizo Mario.

Are there SMW ROM hacks that are not Kaizo hacks? Are there hacks for other video games?
Yes and yes. This site is only for Kaizo Super Mario World hacks. You can find information about other hacks and other games in the Other Resources section.

Why do these games have weird names? What's a "Super Dram World?"
Most games are named after a community figure. Grand Poo World is named after Grand Poo Bear who is a very active member of the Mario community. Super Dram World is named after Dram55 who helped pave the way for what Kaizo has become today.

Kaizo
Literally, Kaizo is a Japanese word which means "rearranged." See FAQ to understand how Kaizo came to mean "really hard Mario levels."

Hack
A ROM of a game with a patch applied to modify the game. A hack could be just a single level, or 10 levels, or 200 levels.

ROM
The game file. When you boot up your Super Nintendo it is reading the cartridge for the ROM file to launch the game.

ASM
Stands for "Assembly for Super Mario." This is custom coding which is applied to the ROM through a patch which allows for things that were not otherwise possible in the original game. Check out this custom Chuck boss in Grand Poo World 2! He's all ASM.

Speed run
An attempt to beat a video game as fast as possible based on certain objective. For example, you can speedrun the original Super Mario World in a couple different ways; maybe you want to get to the credits, or you want to beat every level, or maybe you just need to defeat Bowser. Going as fast as possible to achieve your goal is a speedrun.

TAS
Stands for "tool-assisted speedrun." This is a speedrun that is created with the help of 3rd party tool to achieve absolutely perfect gameplay. These look ridiculous and are pretty impressive feats. These are played back by a computer and are not humanly possible. If something is considered "TAS only" it means that it's achievable but the inputs to achieve the desired result are too difficult for a human.

Input
A button press. When you press "A" on your SNES controller you have "input" "A" into the game. When you hear "frame perfect input" it would suggest that you need to make an input on an exact frame to achieve a desired result (like a wall clip).

Vanilla
An unmodified version of Super Mario World. "Vanilla mechanics" would indicate that the game has no added mechanics that were not in the original SMW.

Chocolate
Modified game mechanics. Mario jumps twice as high by default? Chocolate. Pipes make you fast travel through the same screen instead of taking you to a different screen? Chocolate.

Twitch
A platform for gamers to stream themselves playing video games. You can watch all sorts of games here. Check it out

Troll
A troll is a level or section of a level which attempts to deceive you or kill you and be funny about it. It's hard to make a troll funny without making it entirely unfair.

Kaizo block
A very common troll which is usually just unfair and not funny. A Kaizo block is an invisible block placed near a pit, so that when Mario jump the Kaizo block activates and Mario falls into the pit. Kaizo blocks

Kaizo Light, Kaizo Hard, and Kaizo Pit
These are various grades of "hard" for Kaizo. Light games are achievable by a human player. These are the only Kaizo hacks that are played by humans. Hard games are extremely difficult but technically achievable by a human with the help of emulator tools like savestates or frame advance. Pit Levels are not achievable by a human being; these are TAS-only hacks and are entertaining to watch but impossible to play. Check out Item Abuse.

Savestate
An emulator tool that allows you to save at any time. Want to save after you just made that really hard jump but haven't made the checkpoint yet? Savestates let you do that.

Object
Things that Mario can interact but do not typically interact with Mario. Ground, blocks, water... these are objects. There are exceptions to this, but this definition is good enough for us beginners.

Sprite
Things that Mario can interact with and they can interact with him. P-switches, enemies, Bowser, Koopa shells, powerups... these are sprites.

Hitbox
The hitbox is the area of the sprite that detects whether or not the player or enemy has been hit. For instance, small Mario's sprite is almost twice the size of his hitbox.

Frame rule
The hitbox is the area of the sprite that detects whether or not the player or enemy has been hit. For instance, small Mario's sprite is almost twice the size of his hitbox. Try it yourself - enemies can pass through Mario's head without Mario taking a hit!

Jank
Something that is out of the player's control but is required in order to advance in the level. If a level requires a shell to spawn in a certain area but also allows the player to accidentally spawn the shell early, that's jank. Lots of levels require things to spawn a particular way but have a mechanism to prevent the player from spawning the item early.

Spin Jump
A spin jump allows you to bounce off some enemies that you otherwise cannot bounce off of

Regrab
In SMW, Mario falls slower while the jump button is held. This makes it possible for Mario to travel further in the air by holding the jump button. If you need to let go of jump while in the air (maybe because there are spikes above you and you can't jump to full height) but you still need "slow fall" down, you can achieve this by "regrabbing" the jump button while you're in the air.

P-speed
Stands for Power speed and originating from Super Mario Brothers 3 for the NES, P-speed is the fastest speed at which Mario can run. It's also the speed at which Mario can jump with a cape and begin flying.

"X" Jump (P switch jump, spring jump, shell jump, etc...)
A jump off of "X" item. There are various types of shell jump and certain types of jumps might require frame perfect inputs, but those specifics are more advanced.

Spawn/respawn
The game decides to load an enemy (spawn it) when it is about two tiles (spaces) off screen. Enemies will also unload (despawn) once they are more than about 2 tiles off screen. If Mario doesn't outright kill an enemy (like throwing a Koopa into a pit instead of jumping on it) the enemy will respawn if Mario leaves the area and comes back.

Turnback
Turning Mario away from the direction of travel for a short period of time in order to rapidly reduce his speed
